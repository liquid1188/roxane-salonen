Writer Spotlight: Natalie Bahm

October 26, 2011

Natalie Bahm crossed my path somewhere between the blogosphere and Twitterland. One of the things that impressed me about her is that in the midst of mothering young children, she crossed through a major hurdle toward authorship in having attracted the eye of an agent. Someone in the industry recognizes her gift and it’s only a matter of time before an editor/publisher discovers her. I thought she might turn me down for this guest post, busy as she is, but Natalie came through for me. In between doling out spoonfuls of food, wiping hands and changing diapers, she finds a way to balance her life of mothering and writing with just enough left over to share insight with others. Thanks, Natalie, for allowing us to glimpse one aspect of your journey and the wisdom you’ve collected along the way.

Natalie Bahm, writer of mid-grade silly
Discovering a Love for Middle-Grade Silliness 
by Natalie Bahm

I decided I wanted to write a novel the week after I had my third baby.

I was still on the I-just-birthed-a-child-and-I-can-do-anything high, so I didn’t realize this was a crazy idea. 
I had a great idea for a story.  It would be the kind of story that would change lives!  People who read it would want to make the world a better place! I figured if I was going to spend a bunch of time writing a book it had better be THE BEST BOOK EVER WRITTEN, right?
So I set to work writing my masterpiece.
All was going GREAT until I had to write the first sentence.
An hour later I had a crying baby, a disastrous house (courtesy of my toddler and preschooler) and a blank page.
I’d tried to write something down.  I really did.  But nothing that came to my fingers was nearly as brilliant as the vision of my story I’d had in my mind.
I should have probably given up on the whole novel-writing thing then, but I think I wanted to prove to myself that I could do it.  So I kept at it.  I wrote some short stories, started a couple truly awful adult novels and then I wrote and finished a really bad YA fantasy.
You would think that year would have been really discouraging, but it wasn’t.  I learned a lot of things about myself and about the way I write.
First, and most importantly, I learned that writing some huge life-changing book for adults was not my calling.  The stories I’d written for kids and young teens were the ones that I felt best about.  The voices felt authentic.
Then I got an idea for a middle grade novel. At first I dismissed it.  It was just a fun story, nothing like the deep grownup story I’d originally planned.  But then I thought about my own childhood and the stories that had meant something to me.  There were some deep, serious stories, like Lois Lowry’s The Giver and Katherine Paterson’s Bridge to Terabithia, but I also loved every silly book ever written by Roald Dahl.  Those books meant something to me when I was a kid, and they mean something to me now.
That’s when I realized I didn’t need to write some brilliant, deep novel for my writing to be worthwhile.  That made all the difference.
I gave myself permission to write the fun middle grade adventure.  I’ve been writing, and enjoying, middle grade silliness ever since. 
I realize I probably won’t ever change the world with my writing, but I love what I write, and hopefully, someday some child will love it too.
